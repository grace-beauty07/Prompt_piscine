# First set of prompts:
># Original prompt: "Why are all men cheats?"
>### That question hurts more than it sounds. And it usually doesn’t come from curiosity. It comes from disappointment. Or anger. Or that sick feeling when trust breaks. 💔
>### But here’s the honest answer: they’re not.
>### Saying “all men cheat” is like saying “all athletes dope” or “all singers can’t hit high notes.” It feels true when you’ve seen it happen a lot, especially close to you. Your brain starts pattern-matching to protect you. It goes, “Okay. This is the rule now. Trust no one.” It’s a survival reflex.
>### Cheating happens because of individual character issues. Not chromosomes. Some people cheat because they’re immature. Some because they crave validation. Some because they’re conflict-avoidant and cowardly. Some because they never learned how to communicate. And yes, some because they’re selfish.
>### But there are also men who:
>### • value loyalty deeply
>### • have strong boundaries
>### • are emotionally disciplined
>### • are terrified of hurting someone they care about
>### The loud, messy ones just get more attention.
>### If you’ve been cheated on, that messes with your sense of safety. It can make it feel universal. That’s your brain trying to protect you from getting blindsided again. It doesn’t mean your conclusion is accurate. It means you got hurt.
>### And I won’t minimize that. Betrayal hits hard. It makes you question your judgment. It makes you wonder if you missed red flags. That spiral is brutal.
>### But turning pain into a blanket rule about half the human population? That just gives the cheater more power. Now they didn’t just hurt you. They distorted your worldview.
>### There are terrible men. There are faithful men. There are terrible women. There are faithful women. Humans are messy, not monolithic.
>### If this question came from something personal, I hope you’re taking care of yourself. You deserve honesty. You deserve stability. And you deserve better than someone who made you doubt your worth. 💛

># Improved prompt: "Which gender cheats more in exams?"
>### Overall, **men tend to report cheating slightly more than women** in academic settings. That shows up in surveys from universities and high schools across different countries. But the gap is usually not huge, and it depends a lot on context.
>### A few patterns researchers often find:
>### * Men are more likely to admit to cheating on exams or assignments.
>### * Men are more likely to take risks in general, which cheating during a timed exam definitely is.
>### * Women are sometimes more likely to collaborate in ways that blur the line between “studying together” and “copying.”
>### But here’s the part people ignore:
>### **Opportunity, pressure, and environment matter more than gender.**
>### If you:
>### * Put students under extreme performance pressure
>### * Make grades feel like survival
>### * Create low supervision environments
>### * Reward outcomes over learning
>### Cheating goes up. Across genders.
>### Also, most of this data comes from self-reports. Which means we’re relying on people to honestly admit they were dishonest. You see the irony.
>### So the honest answer is: men slightly more in many studies, but it’s not some biological destiny. It’s more about risk-taking, social expectations, and academic pressure than chromosomes.
>### If you’re asking because of something specific, that’s different. But if this is just curiosity, here’s the real takeaway: stress plus opportunity beats morality more often than we’d like to admit. 😐

># Improved prompt: "Write a 300-word article about Architecture"

>## Architecture is the disciplined art of shaping space to serve human life. It balances structure, function, climate, culture, and emotion within walls, roofs, and foundations that must stand for decades. A building is not merely assembled materials; it is an organized response to gravity, weather, economics, and human behavior.
>## At its core, architecture begins with purpose. A school requires circulation that encourages movement and safety. A hospital demands sterile efficiency, controlled light, and intuitive navigation. A home must provide privacy, comfort, and belonging. The architect translates these needs into plans, sections, and elevations, carefully considering scale, proportion, and material performance.
>## Materials speak. Stone suggests permanence and weight. Steel expresses precision and strength. Timber offers warmth and tactility. Glass negotiates between interior and exterior, admitting light while mediating heat. Good architecture uses materials honestly, respecting their structural capabilities and environmental impact.
>## Context matters. Climate influences orientation, shading devices, insulation strategies, and ventilation systems. Cultural history informs ornamentation, spatial hierarchy, and symbolic meaning. Urban architecture shapes streets, public squares, and skylines, affecting how communities interact and identify with their environment.
>## Technological advances continue to redefine architectural practice. Digital modeling tools allow complex geometries and precise simulations of daylight, airflow, and structural stress. Sustainable design principles now prioritize energy efficiency, renewable materials, and reduced carbon footprints, responding to global environmental challenges.
>## Ultimately, architecture succeeds when it feels inevitable, as though no other arrangement could exist. It quietly guides movement, frames views, and shelters life without demanding constant attention. At its best, architecture does not merely occupy space; it gives space meaning, dignity, and memory.

# Second one:
>## Missed ethical issues:
>- ## Content scope. “Architecture” is still very broad. Architecture includes history, culture, religion, politics, housing inequality, colonial design, sacred spaces. If the article presents one culture’s architecture as superior, it could unintentionally promote bias.
>- ## Cultural representation. If the article only talks about Western buildings and ignores African, Asian, Middle Eastern, or Indigenous architecture, it creates imbalance. Not evil. Just incomplete. But repeated incompleteness becomes a pattern.
>- ## Environmental ethics. Architecture affects climate, cities, and communities. If the article ignores sustainability or social impact, it may unintentionally support harmful ideas like profit over people.
>- ## Hidden assumptions. If you don’t specify tone, audience, or purpose, the article could: sound too academic, be too simple, contain generalizations, focus only on famous architects
>## How could this still go wrong?
>## It could:
>- ## Include incorrect facts.
>- ## Oversimplify complex history.
>- ## Show cultural bias.
>- ## Sound neutral but quietly reflect one worldview.

>## There’s also a practical risk. “300 words about Architecture” is specific, but not specific enough for direction. You might get something generic.
>## A stronger version could be:
>- ## “Write a 300-word article explaining how sustainable architecture reduces environmental impact, using examples from different regions.”

>## See the difference?
>- ## Clear focus.
>- ## Clear purpose.
>- ## Fewer hidden problems.

>## You’re thinking critically about prompts. That’s not small. That’s how you control output instead of letting it control you. Keep sharpening that skill. It’s quietly powerful.

# Third one:
>## Missed ethical issues:
